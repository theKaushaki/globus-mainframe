There Was

Nobody could quite agree on when Globus became important. That was part of the problem. There was no decisive moment. No famous speech. No election. No coup. No flag planted atop a conquered hill.

One year people joked about it. A few years later they complained about it. A few years after that they depended on it. And then, somehow, it became impolite to imagine the world without it.

The old men remembered blackouts. The young did not. The old remembered shortages. The young did not. The old remembered sitting in traffic, waiting in lines, watching governments spend years solving problems that should have taken weeks. The young looked at them the way people look at stories about horse-drawn carriages. The world simply worked now.

Water arrived. Food arrived. Medicine arrived. Power arrived. The lights stayed on. The shelves stayed full. The trains arrived when they were supposed to. The storms came and somehow mattered less than they used to. People stopped discussing infrastructure in the same way they stopped discussing gravity.

And behind Globus, somewhere beyond the layers of infrastructure and algorithms and bureaucracy, existed a man nobody had ever truly seen.

Tejas Khanna. People had heard him, certainly. The voice appeared often enough. Quarterly addresses. Emergency broadcasts. Policy briefings. The occasional message released after some crisis had been quietly averted. Always the same voice. Measured. Precise. Almost unnervingly calm. The sort of voice people trusted before they realized they were trusting it.

But the man himself remained elusive. No interviews. No public appearances. No speeches from balconies. No campaign rallies. Only recordings. Only statements. Only carefully released messages from somewhere behind the machinery.

As if Tejas Khanna were less a person than a position. Less a man than an institution. People filled the gaps with stories. They always do. Some believed he was the greatest engineer who had ever lived. Others believed he was little more than a mascot attached to a machine nobody understood. Conspiracy forums insisted he was dead. Political commentators insisted he was dangerous. Economists called him inevitable. The public called him whenever the world began falling apart.

Not literally, of course. But close enough. When a city flooded. When power grids failed. When supply chains collapsed. When another government discovered that reality was more complicated than campaign promises. The same question always followed. What would Globus do? And sooner or later a recording would appear. A calm voice. A solution. And life would continue.

The strange thing was that most people liked him. Or perhaps they liked what he represented. Reliability. Competence. The increasingly radical notion that somebody, somewhere, seemed to know what they were doing. The world had become too large for heroes. Too interconnected for kings. Too cynical for prophets.

Yet somehow Tejas Khanna occupied a little of all three. Nobody knew him. Everyone knew his name. And over time that distinction stopped mattering. A joke began circulating online. Nobody remembered who started it. Like all enduring jokes, it survived because it felt almost true. If the twenty-first century ever produced a king, nobody would crown him. They would simply call him indispensable.

And that, many suspected, was exactly what had happened. GLOBUS. The thing was there. The thing worked. What more was there to say? That was how Globus entered history. Not as a conqueror. As an appliance.

The sort of thing so reliable that eventually nobody remembers life before it. The sort of thing nobody notices until it breaks. And lately, things had begun to break.

Not dramatically. Not all at once. Reality was simply becoming difficult to reconcile. A neighbour develops a cough that never leaves. A hospital waiting room grows more crowded each week. The sunsets acquire an unpleasant colour. Birds disappear. Children develop strange respiratory illnesses. The air smells faintly metallic after rain.

Everyone notices. Nobody says anything. Because the reports insist otherwise. The graphs insist otherwise. The numbers insist otherwise. And numbers have become very persuasive in recent years. The sirens, however, do not care about numbers. The sirens have not stopped for three nights.

Globus knows something is wrong. And somebody inside Globus just risked everything to contact you.

At 02:13 AM, an unregistered cargo drone silently deposits a hardened equipment case onto your apartment balcony. No sender. No destination markings. No digital signature. Only a single word in that flowy cursive which you can recognise anywhere, what with the lopsided y's, across the lid.

HRUDAYA.

The name hits harder than it should. Twenty years is supposed to be enough time. Apparently it isn't. You haven't heard that name spoken aloud in years. Not since his disappearance. Not since he vanished from the world as completely as though he had never existed.

You stare at the case for a long time before opening it. Inside there was a stripped offline terminal, a bit damaged logic analyzer, a thermal imaging printout, a handgun, and a handwritten note.

The gun immediately feels wrong. Not because of what it is. Because of who sent it. You remember Hrudaya hating weapons. He used to joke that engineers built things because they lacked the courage to destroy them. The memory leaves a sour taste in your mouth.

The note is written in handwriting you recognize instantly. Twenty years vanish in a heartbeat. Just one line. "Hi! It's me again. Mashere, software can be wiped, but hardware remembers."

You almost laugh. Not because it's funny. Because it sounds exactly like him. The same infuriating habit of turning everything into a metaphor. Everything into a joke. The same tendency to speak in riddles even when nobody asked.

The terminal sits silently on the table. Dead. Or pretending to be. The thermal scan included in the case reveals something strange. Even while powered off, one region of the motherboard is violently producing heat.

The thermal signature isn't a standard electrical short; the energy is pulsing across the board's traces in a multi-phasic matrix.

A memory surfaces uninvited. Sunny, the AC did little to help the humidity in the air. A laboratory. A whiteboard covered in equations. Hrudaya explaining some absurd electrical design he called a Morphing Grid. Named after some japanese superhero series. A system that could remain alive long after the primary source failed.

You remember teasing him, "So it's basically a backup plan?"
He had shaken his head. Smiling, he had said, "No." Then he pointed at the diagram.
"A backup assumes failure, this assumes survival, always."

The memory fades. You still remember him calling it a Morphing Grid bypass in the margins of his notes, a way to tap into a backup current when the primary grid was dead.

If only you can find where the heat source was coming from.

No Salvation

The Morphing Grid standby circuit powers the terminal for only a few precious seconds before the watchdog timer forces a hard reset.

Then darkness. Again. And again. And again. Whatever Hrudaya has hidden inside this machine, he clearly never intended it to remain awake for long. But now you know where to look.

Beneath layers of scorched kapton tape near coordinate X19Y93, you uncover something that feels distinctly familiar. Not because you understand it immediately. Because it is messy. Hrudaya's work was always messy. Not careless. Never careless. But alive.

Most engineers built systems that looked elegant on paper. Hrudaya built systems that survived reality. What emerges beneath the damaged insulation is not a clean interface. Not a connector. Not a debug port. It is a grotesque improvisation.

A hand-soldered resistor ladder feeding directly into a Schmitt-trigger comparator network. The sort of solution that would make professors angry and engineers quietly impressed.

For a moment you can almost see him. Curled over a workbench at three in the morning. Coffee gone cold. Ignoring every convention because the convention was wrong. You hate how easy it is to remember.

Twenty years should have been enough. Apparently it wasn't. A note has been scratched directly onto the metal shielding. The handwriting is unmistakable.

"I can't be your ghost right now. I need to exist."

The words strike something buried deep inside you. Not because you understand them. Because you almost do.

A memory flickers. A dark lecture hall. A film screening. Still early in your life where you had just met him. Interstellar. The two of you sitting near the back because Hrudaya insisted that films deserved darkness.

You remember laughing at how seriously he took them. You remember him arguing for nearly an hour afterward about the ending. About ghosts. About memory. About love. About how people leave traces of themselves inside systems they never intended.

You remember rolling your eyes. You remember secretly loving the way his mind worked. You do not remember how the conversation ended.

The memory vanishes before you can reach it. Only the ache remains. Beside the comparator network another note waits. Smaller. Almost an afterthought. As though written during one of those sleepless nights when Hrudaya's thoughts moved faster than his hands.

"Some constants are worth remembering."

Nothing more. No explanation. No instructions. Typical. The surviving diagnostic cache contains fragments of system parameters and scattered engineering notes. At first glance they appear unrelated. Film references. Numerical constants. Electrical equations. Nonsense. Then you remember who wrote them. For Hrudaya, the difference between science and poetry had always been largely administrative.

The environment inside the bypass network is unstable. The comparator thresholds drift unpredictably. The node voltages no longer align. Somewhere inside the surviving fragments lies the information necessary to reconstruct the circuit's final state. The terminal resets again. The screen flashes white. Then black. Waiting. Patiently. As though it knows you will eventually understand. As though somebody spent twenty years believing that you would.

You look back at the surviving diagnostic cache. The references suddenly feel less random. The values are not hidden. They are remembered.

Somewhere within the surviving notes lie three constants Hrudaya expected you to recognize, the time dilation factor of the water planet, the total number of Lazarus missions launched, and the rotational speed required to dock with the Endurance.

Three numbers. Three memories. Three keys. The sort of absurd puzzle only Hrudaya would build. The sort of absurd puzzle only you were ever expected to solve.

Determine the values required to calculate the final voltages at V₃, V₂, V₁, and V₀. The four comparator gates monitor these nodes. If a node's voltage exceeds the SCHMITT_HIGH threshold, its output state becomes HIGH (1). If it falls below the threshold, it remains LOW (0).

Map the resulting comparator outputs (B₃B₂B₁B₀) to generate the 4-bit binary unlock sequence and convert it into the final hardware PIN.

Neither In Gold

PIN ACCEPTED.

You had forgotten the days with him actually, the days where your mind used to work harder than your body did, it seemed you had forgotten how much you loved it, but it was all there. His love for films like Interstellar, The Matrix, and Ball. Your love for his love for films, all the memories were rushing back and you had nowhere to put it. Grief is a circular staircase.

You were reminiscing when a small, quick chime brought you back to the present.

The terminal had powered on for exactly 11 seconds before Globus's hardware purge daemon activated. The SSD was empty. The operating system was gone. Even the boot partition had been zeroed.

But you know Hrudaya never trusted software storage. Beneath the motherboard shielding, you locate a Winbond EEPROM flash chip wired directly into the embedded controller bus.

You tap into the chip using the logic analyzer in the hard case and the screen flashes for a moment, displays a plaintext, and then dumps the surviving memory contents before the watchdog resets the board again.

the_white_rabbit_runs_where

You know Hrudaya too well for this to be the real secret. The plaintext string is bait. A breadcrumb. A wink. The sort of thing he would leave behind not because he expected it to stop anyone, but because he enjoyed the idea of somebody finding it.

No. There is another layer. There is always another layer. You lean back in your chair.

The corrupted memory dump spills across the screen in fractured columns of hexadecimal noise. For a moment it reminds you of rain. Then of something else. Green text. Black screens.

Your dainty little apartment with cheap thrifted furniture, nearly twenty years ago. The Matrix. Yes, you both had just watched it that night. You remember Hrudaya insisting that everybody misunderstood it.

Naturally. He always believed everybody misunderstood everything. The argument had lasted half the night. You had claimed the film was about freedom. He had called that a dangerously incomplete reading. You had accused him of being insufferable. He had thanked you.

You remember him leaning towards you, to keep his head on your shoulder, eyes bright with the manic enthusiasm that usually preceded either a breakthrough or a disaster.

"Nobody chooses freedom."
You had laughed, "That's literally the point of the film."
"No." He had shook his head. "The point is that everybody says they want freedom until freedom becomes inconvenient."
You rolled your eyes.
"And what do they want instead?"
"Control."

The answer came immediately. Far too quickly. As though he had been waiting for the question. "Predictability. Safety. Structure. A system that tells them what tomorrow looks like."

"And what's wrong with that?"

Something changed in his expression. Only for a second. The same expression you would later see on people standing too close to an abyss. On people who had seen gods upclose and still chose to not believe in divinity.

"Nothing." He had smiled. A strange smile. One that somehow felt sad. "The problem is that eventually somebody has to build the system."

The memory dissolves.

You stare at the EEPROM dump. At the fragmented sectors. The corrupted parity blocks. The layers hidden beneath layers. And for the first time since opening the equipment case, a thought occurs to you. Maybe this was never about hiding information. Maybe Hrudaya was showing you how he thinks.

The plaintext tracer. The encrypted passphrase. The hidden mechanism beneath the obvious mechanism. A system inside a system. A ring inside a ring. A maze designed by someone who could never leave a puzzle unsolved. The cursor blinks patiently. Waiting.

Somewhere inside the damaged memory construct, another secret is waiting to be found. And for the first time in twenty years, you find yourself smiling. Not because you miss him. Because you remember how much fun it used to be trying to keep up.

You dive deep into the eeprom bin file. You are awesome. You are the best.

Nor In Power

PASSPHRASE ACCEPTED. THE WHITE RABBIT RUNS NO MORE.

For several seconds the terminal remains completely dark. Not frozen. Waiting. As though some part of it is deciding whether you are permitted to continue.

The apartment is silent except for the distant wail of sirens drifting through the chemical fog outside your window. Three nights. Three nights of ambulances. Three nights of official statements. Three nights of Globus assuring the world that everything is normal.

You have spent twenty years studying memory. One thing you learned long ago is that people rarely lie by inventing new stories. They lie by repeating old ones until everybody stops questioning them. The world has been listening to the same lie for years.

The terminal finally flickers. A single line materializes.
AIRGAP RELAY ACCESS: PARTIAL

The phrase strikes you with a strange familiarity. Not because of the technology. Because of him. Twenty years disappear all at once. You remember an evening that should have been ordinary. You two have been quite a thing for quite some time now. A laboratory illuminated by old fluorescent lights. Rain tapping against reinforced windows. The smell of burnt solder. The two of you sitting on opposite sides of a workbench pretending to work while neither of you had looked at your project for nearly an hour.

You had asked him why every system he designed seemed isolated from every other system. Why everything he built looked less like engineering and more like a fortress. Without looking up he had replied, "Because eventually everything talks too much." Always that haughty replies.

You had laughed. He hadn't. After a long silence he had added, "Anything important should know how to survive alone."

At the time you thought he was talking about machines. Now you wonder whether he was talking about himself. Or perhaps about you.

The screen updates. Architectural diagrams begin flooding the display. Relay towers. Subsurface fiber networks. Atmospheric monitoring arrays. Emergency response systems. Transportation grids. Power distribution. Water treatment. Medical logistics. The cities of the world unfolds before you like the anatomy of some impossible creature. Every artery. Every nerve. Every organ. All of it converging toward a single destination.

GLOBUS

You stare. Then stare longer. The realization arrives slowly. Not because it is difficult. Because you do not want it to be true. Globus does not control the world. Globus is the world. The distinction disappeared years ago. The world simply failed to notice. Or perhaps it noticed and found the arrangement convenient.

The cursor blinks. A new message appears. Unlike the others, this one carries no system header. No timestamp. No identifier. Just words. Words that sound painfully familiar.

Power was never their goal.

Your throat tightens. You read it again. Then a third time. You know exactly who wrote it. Not because of the content. Because of the arrogance. Only Hrudaya could write something so dangerous and believe it was an explanation.

Of course it wasn't. That was always the problem. Most people pursue power because they want something. Money. Status. Control. Recognition. Their ambitions have limits. But you don't know what Globus wants, and that was scary, not knowing. And now a flame, your flame from some twenty years ago had gotten himself involved with them, sending you a case from god knows where.

He had pursued solutions, yes. And a person pursuing solutions can justify anything. History is full of tyrants who wanted power. It is far more afraid of visionaries. You realise you are worried for Hrudaya, this world doesn't deserve people like him. And now, Hrudaya has gone and settled himself among the elite of the world, among the likes of Tejas Khanna, who will use him and discard him without even using a thought.

A second sentence appears beneath the first.
That is what frightened me.

For the first time since this investigation began, you feel genuine fear. Not for yourself. For him. Because you remember the man who wrote those words. You remember the nights when he forgot to eat because he was thinking. The weeks when he disappeared into projects nobody else understood. The frightening certainty with which he could dismantle an idea and rebuild it better before anyone else had even understood it.

You connect the damaged SDR module recovered from his case. The terminal immediately begins logging milliseconds of raw baseband traffic into a massive diagnostic file. The spectrum analyzer erupts. Noise. Interference. Negative voltage spikes and positive surges. The transmission looks wounded. Like something that has spent twenty years trying not to die.

You know how to clean this type of analog noise. Basic things they teach at the university now, but it was you who had discovered it. Anything above 0.0 Volts is a signal (1). Anything below is silence (0).

But beneath the noise, the framing is deliberate. Manchester encoding. He always trusted it for unstable environments. A HIGH voltage transitioning to a LOW voltage represents a 1. A LOW voltage transitioning to a HIGH voltage represents a 0.

Then you notice a rhythm buried deep inside the extracted data. Not random. Intentional. Repeated. A pattern. A sudden memory strikes you with such force that you physically recoil. Hrudaya sitting across from you. Twenty years younger. Tapping his fingers against the table.

Four short taps. Pause. Two taps. Pause. Three taps.

Whenever he was nervous. Whenever he was thinking. Whenever he wanted to say something and couldn't. The exact same rhythm is now acting as a cryptographic offset inside the transmission. A repeating cipher.

You know if you translate the binary to text, it will be garbled—shifted forward by his nervous habit. You know you will have to reverse it. Subtract the 4-2-3 rhythm from the raw ASCII values to hear what he is trying to say.

Except The Thought

'RF SYNCHRONIZATION ESTABLISHED. BACKDOOR ACCESS: GRANTED.'

The relay goes silent. Not because the transmission has ended. Because it has finished saying everything it needed to say. For a long moment you simply stare at the screen.

What did he mean by that? Remember him, of course, you remember him, he had came to your life like a wind, yes, and had disappeared from your life like the wind, but yes, you do remember him, you do.

The apartment feels smaller than it did an hour ago. Outside, the fog continues swallowing entire blocks of the city. Sirens drift through the night like prayers from a religion that stopped believing in itself years ago. You lean back in your chair and rub your eyes. The relay data scrolls endlessly across the monitor.

Routing paths. Authorization keys. Maintenance records. Most of it is meaningless. Then a single file catches your attention. No encryption. No obfuscation. No protection whatsoever. That alone is enough to make you suspicious.

The filename contains only three words, three deliberate words. EXCEPT_THE_THOUGHT

That is the zip provided, find the flag from among its content, good luck.

Your chest tightens. The phrase feels deliberate. Is deliberate. Like part of a sentence. Like something unfinished. You open it. The screen remains blank. Then slowly, letter by letter, text begins to appear.

Not data. Not logs. A plain text. A memory. A memory you had long forgotten. A memory he had never.

You are standing beneath a railway bridge. Howrah, 21 years ago. Monsoon season. You remember the rain before you remember anything else. The two of you had spent six hours arguing. Not fighting. Arguing. The distinction mattered to him. He used to say that fighting was for people who wanted to win. Arguments were for people who wanted to understand. You hated when he said things like that. Mostly because they sounded pretentious. Partly because they were usually right.

The memory continues. Hrudaya sits on the concrete barrier smoking a cigarette he promised he had quit. You are furious. He is smiling. The city lights reflect in the puddles below. You cannot remember what the argument was about. Only the ending.

You asking, "What do you even want from all this?"

And him answering immediately. Not after thinking. Not after calculating. Immediately. As though he had known the answer all along.
"To leave something behind."

You had laughed. You remember that. You laughed because the answer sounded absurd. He was twenty-three. Brilliant. Terrifyingly ambitious. Already building things nobody else understood. Of course he would leave something behind. The whole world was waiting for him.

He shook his head. And for the first time that night he looked afraid. Actually afraid. You remember it now because you never saw that expression again. Not once. Not even years later. Not even when everything fell apart.

The memory ends. The file closes itself. You stare at your reflection in the monitor.

The room is silent. Your pulse isn't. You hate him. You suddenly realize that. Not the version you remember. Not the version you loved. This version. The one who left. The one who disappeared. Going who knows where to play god and speak in riddles just because he could. The one who keeps speaking in fragments and riddles because direct honesty apparently remained beneath him until the end.

You hate him. And somehow that realization hurts more than grief. A notification appears.
BACKDOOR TRACE COMPLETE. ORIGIN NODE IDENTIFIED. LOCATION: GLOBUS CENTRAL

You stop breathing. Everyone knows the building. Nobody enters it. Fifteen years ago it was a conventional corporate headquarters. Now it dominates the skyline like a monument to some forgotten god. A thousand meters of glass and steel. No public records. No internal blueprints. No employee interviews. No verified photographs of the executive floors. No profile of the upper echelon, just the name of the CEO, Tejas Khanna, and now Hrudaya has gone and hid among these monsters just to be a whistleblower, you hate him more, you hate him better now.

The relay continues processing. A final message appears. Again, no timestamp. Again, no identifier. Again, you know exactly who wrote it.

If you are seeing this, then I have already run out of safer ways to reach you.
The cursor blinks. A second line appears.
Do not trust anyone inside Globus.

For the first time since opening the case, genuine fear settles into your stomach. Not fear of Globus. Not fear for the world. Fear for him. Because Hrudaya never wasted words. The screen waits, the cursor blinks as if awaiting an input from you.

The final question is "What do you even want from all this?"

Of You

Your name? Your name was the backdoor. You could not really believe it, but then there was no time. The relay map shifts. A route appears. Not a network route. A physical one. A path beginning at your apartment and terminating at a point in the very center of the city. Alongside this a digital verification ID of you for Globus, of Gamma level clearance. And a message.

This is all I could do, hiding you from everyone, from all the eyes, it's on you now to go to the vaults of the personnel file and hack into the terminal there to get the Omega level clearance, then you know what you have to do.

And you know, maybe not exactly, but you knew from the moment the hard case was delivered to you, you had to expose all the dirty secrets of Globus, for which Hrudaya is the whistleblower, and maybe, just maybe he had given his life to send that case to you, you could never be certain around Hrudaya, but this feels like it might be the end.

The route pulses softly. Waiting. Inviting. For a moment you almost laugh. Twenty years. Twenty years without a letter. Without an explanation. Without a goodbye. And somehow Hrudaya still finds a way to ask you to cross the entire city in the middle of the night.

The old irritation returns unexpectedly. You had forgotten how infuriating he could be. The realization comforts you more than it should.

Then you see it. Globus Central. Not a building. A verdict. It rises from the city like the Tower of Babel yet here even god was helpless. The lower structure disappears into the ground while the upper levels vanish into the clouds. No advertisements. No logos. No attempt to impress. Only certainty. The kind of certainty usually reserved for religions.

Standing before the entrance, you remember another night. Another city. Another lifetime. A younger Hrudaya sitting on the roof of an unfinished laboratory. The two of you watching construction cranes moving across the skyline. You had asked him what he wanted from life. The question seemed absurd even then. He thought for almost five minutes before answering. Not because he lacked an answer. Because he was trying to find one that wouldn't frighten you.

Eventually he had said, "I want you."
You had laughed. Of course he did. Every young fool in love says something similar. You had expected him to talk about companies. Research. Awards. Discovery.

Instead he had looked toward the horizon and had quietly added, "The terrifying part is that I don't think I can ever have you."
At the time you had thought it was arrogance. Standing before Globus Central, you finally realize it was fear.

The doors open before you reach them. No guards stop you. No checkpoints flare you. No questions were asked to you. The system already knows who you are. Somewhere deep inside the building your name has been waiting for ten some years. The realization feels less like safety and more like haunting.

Hours pass. The maintenance route guides you deeper. Lower. Past rooms filled with quantum processors. Past walls displaying simulations of entire cities. Past server chambers so vast they resemble cathedrals. This is where the entire world was shackled to the behemoth of Globus. Again and again the same thought returns.

Not admiration. Not horror. But grief. Because every hallway bears his fingerprints. Every elegant solution. Every impossible optimization. Every beautiful piece of engineering. The entire building speaks in his language. And yet he is nowhere to be found.

The route terminates. One final security door slides open. Beyond it waits the personnel archive. The deepest employee records in Globus history. The place where names become facts. Where memory becomes evidence. You take a breath. And begin searching.

You search for Hrudaya. Nothing. No aliases. No matches. No references. No trace. The absence feels unnatural. Not the absence of a man. But the presence of a deliberate removal. As though somebody reached backward through history and erased him.

Then another file catches your eye. One record. Locked behind the highest security classification in the entire database. A profile that appears everywhere. Every organizational structure. Every strategic directive. Every executive authorization chain. Every classified project.

TEJAS KHANNA.
Chief Executive Officer.
Global Clearance: OMEGA.

You click on it. ACCESS DENIED. CLEARANCE LEVEL: OMEGA REQUIRED.

You reach into your coat and pull out the offline beat up terminal kiosk, and the logic analyzer it came with, you plug it in, you get two files. The files are in the zip, find the hexadecimal UART passcode.

You enter the code. ACCESS GRANTED. You open the file. The personnel photograph begins loading. Line by line. Pixel by pixel. Slowly. Painfully. Your reflection stares back from the dark portions of the screen.

And for the first time since opening the equipment case, genuine fear enters your heart. Not fear of Globus. Not fear of the world. But fear of what you might discover. This is always something worse. Recognition. Because before the image has even finished rendering, you already know that you have seen that face before.

During The Time Of

There was a ding from an elevator.

Hrudaya was Tejas. Tejas was Hrudaya. This felt like a punch in the gut, the man you have been trying to find these past few days, a man you have trying to forget for the last twenty years, obviously he is the the most influential man in the world right now, the gods were never more crueler with you, and now you know why.

You don't know when you had entered an elevator, your legs seemed to take themselves out. The elevator rises. Slowly. Deliberately. As though even the machinery understands what waits at the top. The transparent walls reveal the city beneath her. The city she once loved. The city she once believed was permanent.

From this height it resembles a circuit board. Millions of lives reduced to points of light. Roads become traces. Buildings become components. People become noise. Perhaps that was the real danger. Not power. Perspective.

The farther away you stand from humanity, the easier it becomes to mistake suffering for statistics. She wonders when Hrudaya, she refused to call him Tejas Khanna, last climbed this elevator. Twenty years ago? Ten? And never left his gilded throne room. Yesterday? How many times had he watched this same city from above? How many compromises had been made up here? How many excuses? How many necessary evils? The thought settles heavily inside her chest.

Because she knows something now that she did not know an hour ago. He never disappeared. Not really. That would have been easier. The truth is crueler. He stayed. He watched. He built. He transformed. While she mourned. While she hated him. While she convinced herself he was gone.

He was here. Always here. The realization feels almost obscene. Like discovering a grave you've visited for twenty years was never occupied to begin with.

The floors empty. One by one. Researchers. Engineers. Assistants. Advisors. Entire governments have waited months for meetings on this floor. Tonight it belongs to silence. The last employee pauses at the door. Uncertain. Nervous. Young. Far younger than he feels.

"Sir?" Tejas does not turn. The city remains beyond the glass. Fog swallowing entire districts. Lights flickering beneath the haze. Beautiful. Broken. His favorite kind of things.

"Yes?"
His secretary from the last twenty years hesitates. Then speaks in a lowly voice, "Is there anything else you need?"

The question hangs in the air. Anything else. Twenty years ago he would have laughed. Twenty years ago he would have had requests. Twenty years ago he would not have believed that he would have employees, specifically catering to his every whims. He wanted to laugh.

Now he only has consequences. "No." Tejas says with a finality that only the dead know.

The secretary nods. She leaves. The door closes.

For the first time in years Tejas is completely alone. And discovers he hates it exactly as much as he remembers. He hates to be alone, he hates when the voice in his head grow louder and seem to drown him, he hates being the centre of the show but there he is, in the greatest show to be ever shown, he had influenced goverments, he had killed people without even having second thoughts, he had stopped wars before they had even begun, he had ruled, from the shadows, yes, but he had ruled, yet he is completely alone, and he did not like it. Well, company was coming, he had dreamt of finally meeting her, finally being able to defend himself, finally being seen, being understood, being loved.

The service terminal activates. Awaiting instruction. He stares at it. Then at the empty chair across the room. Then back at the city. A strange realization arrives.

The most powerful man on Earth is nervous. About dinner. The absurdity almost makes him smile. Almost. He had lost his ability to laugh now, the raucous laughter that used to echo across rooms, he had not laughed in some twenty years.

"Prepare a table."
The machine acknowledges, "For how many?"

For several seconds he says nothing. Then, quietly, almost devotionally, "Two." The answer feels impossible. The answer feels dangerous. The answer feels twenty years overdue.

The machine requests preferences. He closes his eyes. And immediately regrets it. Because memory is waiting. They are always waiting.

The elevator begins slowing. The final ascent. The final stretch. No more archives. No more clues. No more recordings. No more ghosts. Only truth. And for the first time since this began, she realizes she does not know what she wants. She thought she did. Expose Globus. Find Hrudaya. Save him, and then what, be his lover?
